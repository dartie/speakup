King Donald I: The First American Monarchy? [B2]

Il per due volte presidente degli USA guida il Paese come se fosse il CEO di una grande azienda, ma con ambizioni da re. In tutto questo, c’è spazio per la democrazia?

Democracy is obsolete, they think. As a governing system, it is fundamentally flawed, inefficient and burdensome. It’s a gruelling process with all of its convincing the public to vote for one candidate (never mind the primaries and campaign finance regulations,) followed by endless debates, legislative compromises and institutional checks. Worst of all, democracy is bad for business. This thesis is not new, but it is gaining traction among certain economic and political elites. And as the oldest democracy and still the most powerful country in the world, the United States is also leading the way into new forms of government.

THE CULTURE WARS

When Trump first came to power, some argued he was the figurehead of a new kind of fascism. Indeed, he had triumphed by tapping into the discontent of a large segment of the working class who felt betrayed by the political and cultural establishment. By stoking the fear of immigration and aligning with religious zealots, the alt-right and white supremacists in the culture wars, Trump became a trailblazer for global conservatism, a model for aspiring authoritarians seeking to exploit years of economic crisis and political unrest. The fascist argument is not entirely baseless, but less than a year into Donald Trump’s second term, there are signs of a different kind of post-democratic project; something between a corporation and a monarchy, where power is centralised and exercised with little or no democratic oversight.

A KING OR A CEO

When the real estate tycoon and the author of The Art of the Deal announced his political ambitions in 2015, he pledged to “manage the government like a business.” Trump’s fondness for monarchies, meanwhile, has been manifest throughout his life: from his love of gold and regal décor to branding all his buildings with his name, or rather that of the American dynasty inaugurated by his father.  Although kings rule countries and CEOs run corporations, there are striking similarities between these two forms of authority. Both rely on strong, top-down leadership. Both aim to expand their dominance: the latter by overpowering the competition and acquiring market share; the former by annexing territory, as Trump has suggested doing with Greenland, the Panama Canal and even Canada.

ABOVE THE LAW

Historically, kings have stood above the law. And the 45th and 47th President of the United States believes, as he said shortly after winning his second term, that the popular mandate came with “the right to do what we wanna do to make our country great again.” A few months later, he insisted by posting a quote on social media, allegedly from none other than Napoleon: “He who saves his country does not violate any law.” But making America great again is a tall order, and the US Constitution limits presidents to two terms, allowing for little time to accomplish his agenda. In the first hundred days of his second administration, Trump signed a record-breaking 143 executive orders, thereby overriding the approval of Congress and eroding the system of checks and balances established in the Constitution. In July, he managed to pass the so-called Big Beautiful Bill, a legislative package that extended tax cuts, deregulated key industries and expanded executive powers. Some see this movement as one of the first steps of Project 2025, a blueprint for a post-democratic, CEO-style presidency that aims to reshape the federal government into a streamlined, loyalty-based apparatus.

DARK ENLIGHTMENT

Still, there is a growing belief among MAGA radicals that the Constitution should be amended to allow for a third term. But even that may not be enough for those who believe democracy is nothing short of mob rule. Over the past few years, a movement calling itself the Dark Enlightenment — or the Neo-reactionary movement — has advocated a return to ancient forms of government, such as absolute monarchy. Its leading theorist, Curtis Yarvin, has influenced a number of Silicon Valley elites, most notably billionaire investor Peter Thiel. Thiel played a pivotal role in the rise of J. D. Vance, Trump’s vice president, hiring him at his venture capital firm and later becoming the main donor in Vance’s pursuit of a political career.

ALL HAIL THE KING

But it’s not just elites with vested interests who are turning away from democracy. Decades of economic stagnation and political corruption have eroded public trust in the institutions that were once considered pillars of democracy, namely the justice system, the mainstream media, public schools and universities and even science. To many on the right, this has built the case for a Trump dynasty, which, like the monarchies of the Ancien Régime, is rooted in a quasi-religious cult of personality. The superstructure legitimising Trump has been built on a social media ecosystem rife with fake news, climate change denial and ludicrous conspiracy theories like QAnon, that portray the President as a messiah who plays “4-D Chess” with the rest of the world, urging followers to “trust the plan” and dismiss all criticism. Next year, the American Republic will celebrate its 250th anniversary. One of the few shared beliefs among Americans is that the United States became a nation by rebelling against the tyranny of the English monarchy.
