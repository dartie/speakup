The Penny: Is This The End? [B1]

I costi di produzione di un penny sono più alti del suo valore nominale. Vale la pena continuare a produrre questa moneta? Secondo il presidente Trump, no.

Every day, the US Mint produces about thirty million pennies, which cost over three times more to produce than they’re worth. This year, US President Donald Trump instructed the Department of the Treasury to end the production of the penny. However, it’s not yet clear if the coin will really be discontinued or not.

HEADS OR TAILS?

The US penny, officially known as ‘the cent’, was first produced in 1787, and has been issued primarily as a copper or copper-plated coin. Since 1909, the year President Abraham Lincoln would have turned a hundred, the coin has featured President Lincoln’s profile.  Modern pennies feature the president’s profile on the obverse (heads) of the coin and a Union Shield on the reverse (tails.) The thirteen stripes on the shield represent the thirteen original states, and the bar across the top represents Congress and the Federal Government.

EXPENSIVE METAL

The penny has been the lowest-value unit of US currency since the half-cent coin was discontinued in 1857. In the early 2010s, the price of the metal used to make pennies increased significantly.  The most recent report from the US Mint says that each penny costs 3.7 cents to make, including three cents for production, and 0.7 cents for administrative and distribution costs. Because of inflation, pennies have lost practically all their purchasing power, and so are often perceived as a nuisance by institutions and people in general. 

HOW DO YOU SAY…?

Un sacco di soldi.  A pretty penny.

SO WASTEFUL!

In February, President Trump said on his Truth Social platform that he had instructed the Treasury Secretary, Scott Bessent, to end production of the penny, which he described as “so wasteful.” “Let’s rip the waste out of our great nation’s budget, even if it’s a penny at a time,” he wrote.  The US will not be the first country to eliminate the penny. New Zealand and Australia ended the production of their pennies back in 1989 and 1992, respectively, and Canada ended production of its penny in 2012.

WHAT ABOUT THE NICKEL?

The abolition of the US penny is a big deal and will probably require congressional approval. This is because if the US eliminates its penny, it will have to produce more of its second-lowest-value coin: the five-cent coin, or ‘nickel’. But nickels cost even more to produce than pennies, at 13.8 cents. So, perhaps the US penny is better left alone, at least for now!
