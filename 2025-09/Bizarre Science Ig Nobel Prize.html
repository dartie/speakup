Bizarre Science: Ig Nobel Prize [B1]

È arrivato il momento della cerimonia di consegna degli Ig Nobel, i premi assegnati ai dieci più insoliti, bizzarri e irrilevanti risultati scientifici di quest’anno!

Scientific achievements often amaze or fascinate us. Then there are some, such as the discovery that mammals can potentially breathe through their anuses or that dead trout can swim, that are so unexpected, bizarre or downright dubious that it makes us laugh in disbelief and delight. It is these type of achievements that are celebrated at the 35th annual Ig Nobel Prize ceremony held at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) in Cambridge, Massachusetts in September. 

IGNOBLE AWARDS

Created in 1991, the Ig Nobel (a play on the word ‘ignoble’) Prize ceremony is organised by the scientific humour magazine Annals of Improbable Research (AIR). It awards ten prizes that “honour achievements so surprising that they make people LAUGH, then THINK,” according to AIR. The prizes are intended to celebrate the unusual, honour the imaginative — and spur people’s interest in science, medicine and technology.

HOW DO YOU SAY…?

Rendersi ridicolo. To make a fool of yourself.

PLEASE STOP, I’M BORED!

Nobel laureates (mostly) present the awards, which come in the form of banknotes for ten trillion Zimbabwean dollars (equivalent to less than 50 cents.) The winners then give a public lecture. But if they speak for too long, they are interrupted by Miss Sweetie Poo, a little girl who repeatedly shouts “Please stop, I’m bored!” This is one of several eccentric traditions at the ceremony, which concludes with the announcement “If you didn’t win a prize — and especially if you did — better luck next year!”

PAST WINNERS 

One of the most famous winners of the satirical science award is Sir Andre Geim, who is the only person ever to have won an Ig Nobel Prize and a Nobel Prize. He won the Ig Nobel Prize in 2000 for levitating a frog by magnetism, and a Nobel Prize in 2010 for his work on the electromagnetic properties of graphene. The work of fictional scientists has also been honoured.  Real-life past winners include Michigan-based televangelists Jack and Rexella Van Impe, for their claim that black holes meet all the technical requirements to be the location of hell; British poultry farmers, for their discovery that ostriches are sexually attracted to humans; and high-school student Jillian Clarke for her research exploring the belief that food dropped on the floor can be safely eaten if picked up within five seconds — a belief colloquially known as the ‘Five-Second Rule’.   www. improbable.com
